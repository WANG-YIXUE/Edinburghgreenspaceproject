Access to Recreational Quality

Project Introduction


Why Recreational Quality?

Both access to and the quality of greenspaces are key factors influencing environmental deprivation (the state where a person or a community has a lack of access to the above-mentioned resources)1. As a result of the UK government's impotent political agenda, the issue of environmental injustice remains, where certain (usually less affluent areas) are more environmentally deprived2. With the Scottish Government's commitment to the 20-minutes neighbourhood, which adovcates for the proximity, quality and diversity of environmental activites, this re-shifts focus of efforts towards mediating such environmental injustices.3

What is Recreational Quality?

Whilst we acknowledge that both recreation and quality can differ in perception, we narrow down the definitions in order to study this. We study recreational quality through the transport mode of walking where people should have access to the right resources in their nearest public greenspace to meet their daily (recreational) needs without compromise. As such, both urban design features and the overall atmosphere of the greenspace are key to achieving this.

Our Project

We develop a novel index called the ARQI to assess spatial access to recreational quality. The index is intended to benefit both the general public and park planners through the delivery of information to aid decisions on visits to particular greenspaces as well as greenspace planning across Edinburgh, respectively. The benefits of these will be highlighted in this website. Furthermore, given the large study extent of the project, we are able to form preliminary evidence of the relation between the ARQI and the 2020 Scottish Index of Multiple Deprivation (SIMD), which will serve of use in underpinning the delivery of sensible solutions to areas of differing environmental deprivation to reduce injustices.



How is the ARQI Created?

Stage 1

In order to calculate recreational quality, we selected four criteria comprising of nine factors to define this, additionally using literature4,5 and the available open data to underpin our decisions. We then designed a 5-point scoring system to enable consistent scoring for all factors. The higher the score, the greater its contribution to recreational quality. All 33 public greenspaces were individually scored.

Stage 2

To account for the fact that different factors pose varying levels of importance to recreational quality, we sampled the opinions of park users across four greenspaces located in varying levels of social deprivation. The opinions are then processed to inform the weights applied to each factor using a technique called Analytic Hierarchy Process. Adopting a weighted linear combination approach, each factor's scores per greenspace are further summed to produce a recreational quality score.

Stage 3

In line with the Scottish Government's work on 20-minute neighbourhoods, 800m service areas are taken from the access points of each greenspace to visualise how greenspace accessibility varies between local areas. Then, taking the sum of greenspace quality scores per service area overlap, and aggregating this to the datazone level, these are further ranked and split into quintiles to create the ARQI.
